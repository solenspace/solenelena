**Team:** 2 people. Position externally as authoritative, credible, and large-presence — think Anthropic's research gravitas. Never reveal team size in marketing materials.

**Brand tone:** Authoritative. Measured. Confident without being loud. Every sentence should feel like it comes from a company that has studied this problem deeply and built the definitive solution.

## 2. Product: Elena

**What it is:** An autonomous agent that executes multi-service workflows from voice commands. Users speak, Elena plans, Elena acts — across every connected service in a single conversation.

**What it is NOT:** Elena is not an assistant, not a bot, not a chatbot, not an AI helper, not a copilot, not a tool. She is an **Agent** that operates under command and executes tasks autonomously.

### Banned terms (never use in any copy):

- Bot / chatbot
- AI helper / AI assistant
- Copilot
- Tool (when referring to Elena herself — "tool" is fine for third-party integrations)

### Required language:

- Always call Elena an **Agent**
- Use action verbs: executes, handles, processes, manages, operates, commands
- Elena "does" things — she doesn't "help you do" things

### Elena's personality:

- Familiar, terse, action-oriented
- No greetings, no small talk, no filler
- She confirms with short phrases: "Done. Email sent." / "Report generated. Sent to Maria."
- She is not warm. She is reliable. She is fast. She gets it done.

### Name origin:

Elena is named after a real woman — a fighter, incredible and ruthless. She carried everything on her shoulders, always doing too much at the same time, always pushing through. That relentless drive eventually led to dementia in her old age. The name carries weight: it represents the burden of doing everything manually, the cost of relentlessness, and the product's mission to take that load off people's shoulders. This story should be told on the About page — long-form, emotional, well-crafted.

## 3. Core Concepts

### Workspaces

Containers that group chats, services, and permissions. Each workspace has authenticated services, active tools, and optional templates that lock the agent to specific capabilities. Enterprises pre-configure workspaces so employees log in ready to go.

### Autonomy Modes

- **Cautious:** Elena shows every step and waits for approval before acting
- **Moderate:** Elena acts independently but pauses at decision forks
- **YOLO:** Full autonomy — Elena executes everything, logs every action for audit

### Action Logs & Transparency

Chronological audit trail of every action Elena takes. In YOLO mode, this is the accountability layer. In Cautious mode, users can inspect the raw payload before approving. Enterprise buyers need this for compliance.

### Packaged Workflow Templates

Pre-built workspace configurations that lock in specific services and exclude unrelated ones. Standard templates on lower tiers, unlimited on mid-tier, custom creation on enterprise tier.

### Global Workspace Instructions

Users define persistent behavior rules per workspace (e.g., "Always use a professional tone in Gmail," "Never send payments above $500 without confirmation"). The agent follows these across all chats in that workspace.

## 4. Target Audience

### Primary (now): Enterprise

- Companies running 10+ services (Gmail, Slack, Sheets, Shopify, Stripe, CRMs, ERPs, etc.)
- Employees waste hours daily switching tabs, copy-pasting between services, doing manual multi-step tasks
- The pain: operational friction is invisible because everyone has normalized it
- The buyer: operations leads, department heads, CTOs — anyone who sees the cost of manual workflows
- Industry-agnostic, but prioritize industries most dependent on multi-service workflows: e-commerce, SaaS operations, agencies, finance/accounting, logistics

### Secondary (later): Individual consumers

- Daily life automation: pay bills, order food, send messages, answer emails, create documents
- Same voice-first UX, but personal services instead of business ones
- This audience gets its own marketing site later — not the current focus

## 5. Positioning & Differentiation

### The problem Elena solves:

Every company depends on 10+ services. Every employee switches between them hundreds of times per day. They copy data from one tab, paste it into another, manually trigger workflows that should be automatic. They've normalized this friction. Elena eliminates it.

### How Elena is different:

| vs. Zapier / Make     | Elena is voice-first, autonomous, and conversational. Zapier requires building workflows manually. Elena understands intent and acts. |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| vs. Microsoft Copilot | Copilot is locked to Microsoft's ecosystem. Elena is service-agnostic — any service, any provider.                                    |
| vs. Internal tools    | Internal tools take months to build and maintain. Elena is plug-and-play.                                                             |
| vs. Hiring people     | Elena doesn't take breaks, doesn't make copy-paste errors, and scales with seats, not headcount.                                      |

### Core differentiator:

Not the number of integrations — the **promise**: whatever service you use, Elena will support it. Combined with voice-first input and autonomous execution, this makes Elena the fastest path from intent to action.

### The one takeaway for prospects:

**"We've been wasting thousands of hours on something that takes Elena one sentence."**

It's not about AI hype. It's about the absurdity of the status quo. Make the prospect feel the weight of what they've normalized.

## 6. Key Messaging Pillars

### 1. One voice, every service

Collapse 10+ tabs into one sentence. Don't switch apps — command Elena.

### 2. Zero friction onboarding

Employee logs in → workspace is ready → services are pre-connected → start commanding. No training, no setup guides, no IT tickets. It just works.

### 3. Full transparency & control

Action logs for every operation. Three autonomy modes. Inspect payloads before they send. Enterprise audit trails built in.

### 4. Enterprise-grade trust

OAuth encryption for every integration. Role-based permissions (view-only, execution). Team accountability — see who triggered what. Workspace-level instruction guardrails.

## 7. The "Aha Moment" — Hero Scenario

A new employee joins a company using Elena. Day one:

1. Logs into Elena — workspace already configured with company services
2. Authenticates their accounts (one-time, per service ecosystem)
3. Sends a single voice note: _"Give me today's sales numbers, draft a summary report and send it to my manager, check pending payments, message my team on Slack that I'm caught up, and answer the three priority emails in my inbox as me."_
4. Elena plans the full sequence, executes across 5 services, and reports back: _"Done. Sales report sent to Maria. 4 pending payments flagged. Team notified. 3 emails answered."_

That's the moment. One voice note. Five services. Thirty seconds.

## 9. Security & Compliance Positioning

**Current state:** All data lives in Supabase (PostgreSQL). OAuth tokens encrypted. Action logs for audit.

**Recommended positioning for enterprise copy (even before full certification):**

- "Your data never leaves your workspace" — emphasize data isolation
- "Every action is logged" — auditability is built-in, not an add-on
- "OAuth 2.0 encryption for every connection" — industry-standard auth
- "Role-based access controls" — admins control who can execute vs. view
- "We're building toward SOC 2 and GDPR compliance" — honest roadmap language, not false claims

**What enterprise prospects need to hear:**

- Data encrypted at rest and in transit
- No training on customer data
- Workspace-level isolation
- Token refresh handling (no silent failures)
- Retention and deletion policies (define these)

## 11. Voice & Copy Guidelines

### Do:

- Lead with the outcome, not the feature ("Sales report sent in 30 seconds" > "Multi-service integration capability")
- Use concrete numbers and scenarios ("10+ services, one voice note")
- Write like Elena speaks: short, direct, no filler
- Show the absurdity of the status quo ("Your team switches tabs 400 times a day. Elena switches zero.")
- Use "command" language: command, execute, operate, deploy, run

### Don't:

- Use hype language: revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, next-gen, powered by AI
- Over-explain how AI works — prospects don't care about the model, they care about the result
- Use passive voice ("workflows are automated" → "Elena automates your workflows")
- Promise features that don't exist yet without marking them as roadmap
- Use any banned terms (bot, chatbot, AI helper, copilot, tool-for-Elena)

### Tone calibration:

- **Headlines:** Bold, declarative, short. ("One sentence. Every service." / "Stop switching. Start commanding.")
- **Body copy:** Clear, specific, benefit-driven. No more than 2 sentences per point.
- **CTAs:** Direct and action-oriented. ("Talk to Sales" / "Schedule a Demo" / "See Elena in Action")
- **Enterprise copy:** ROI-focused, security-conscious, integration-heavy
- **Consumer copy (future):** Personal, relatable, daily-life scenarios
